# An upgrade-council module for backup token upgrades

`XanGovernor` + `TimelockController` own and upgrade the token (see [ADR-06](./06-governance-agnostic-owner-only-upgradeability.md)). To let an **upgrade council** (a Safe multisig) act as a **backup upgrade path** when the voter body is inactive, we add a dedicated **`XanUpgradeCouncilModule`** module rather than widening the token or the Governor.

The token stays owner-only and the timelock stays its owner. The module holds the timelock's `PROPOSER` and `CANCELLER` roles and never owns the token: it *schedules* an `upgradeToAndCall` operation into the timelock, and the timelock — as `owner()` — executes it, satisfying the token's `onlyOwner` `_authorizeUpgrade`. The module's single authority is the **council multisig**, fixed at deployment as an immutable (`getCouncil()`) and guarded by one modifier, `onlyCouncil`. The council holds no timelock roles; its only on-chain power is calling the module's council-gated functions.

The council's powers are deliberately minimal — **subordination to the voter body is one-way**:

- **Propose: upgrades only.** `scheduleUpgrade(newImplementation, data)` (council-gated) is the only way the council schedules, and it can only ever build a single `upgradeToAndCall` on the token. The delay is **computed live** as `votingDelay() + votingPeriod() + timelock.getMinDelay() + buffer`, so the cancel window always exceeds a full voter cancel cycle even if Governor settings change. Only one council upgrade may be in flight at a time.
- **Cancel: its own pending upgrade only.** `cancelUpgrade()` (council-gated) withdraws the module's own pending upgrade operation — nothing else. The module holds the timelock's `CANCELLER` role, but its code only ever aims it at the operation id the module itself scheduled. The council has **no cancel power over voter-body operations**.

Two upgrade paths, both landing as a timelocked `upgradeToAndCall`:

- **Voter body** — a normal `XanGovernor` proposal (vote → queue → timelock delay → execute).
- **Council upgrade** — `council → XanUpgradeCouncilModule.scheduleUpgrade(...)`, waiting out the full cancel window.

**The voter body cancels a council upgrade** via a `XanGovernor` proposal that relays `TimelockController.cancel(operationId)` through the governor, which holds the timelock's `CANCELLER` role. This reuses the Governor's quorum (no bespoke vote-counting) — which is *why* the council delay must out-size the voter cancel cycle.

**Replacing the council.** The council address is **immutable** — the module has no rotation function. A Safe changes its own signers internally without changing its address, so routine membership changes need no on-chain action. Changing the multisig _address_ means deploying a fresh module, granting it the timelock's `PROPOSER`/`CANCELLER` roles, and revoking the old module's — all through voter-body proposals. That role revocation is also how the voter body disarms a captured council, so the immutable design loses no capability the voter body did not already have. Because the council can cancel nothing but its own upgrade, **voter supremacy is structural** — no carve-out machinery is needed to protect against a captured council.

## Considered options

- **Extend `XanGovernor` with V1-style council functions** — rejected: bloats and de-standardises the already-complex Governor and enlarges the re-audit surface on every governance change.
- **Module owns the token directly** (a literal V1 port) — rejected: duplicates the timelock's delay/cancel machinery, moves ownership off the timelock the rest of governance uses, and double-delays voter-body upgrades.
- **Bare timelock roles for the council, no module** — rejected: `PROPOSER` is not operation-scoped, so the council could schedule *any* action; the module exists to keep the *propose* path upgrades-only on-chain.
- **A general cancel power for the council (mutual check)** — the earlier design: `cancel`/`cancelBatch` over any queued operation (bar a standalone-rotation carve-out) as an emergency brake against a malicious voter-body proposal. Rejected on **capture-cost asymmetry**: passing a malicious voter proposal requires quorum — currently 50% of a voting supply that includes locked balances (see [ADR-03](./03-voting-power-tracks-full-balance.md)), with timestamp-checkpointed votes killing flash-loan capture — while the brake was operable by compromising a handful of multisig keys. The brake therefore defended against the astronomically expensive attack while itself *being* the cheap attack surface: a captured council could DoS every governance operation except its own rotation. It also dragged in the rotation carve-out and its bundling edge cases. (This argument weakens if the quorum parameter is lowered before deployment — re-evaluate it then.)
- **No cancel at all** (module drops `CANCELLER` entirely) — rejected: the council could not withdraw its own mistakenly scheduled upgrade in the exact scenario the module exists for — a dormant voter body, the only other canceller. With the executor role open, a flawed council upgrade would be guaranteed to execute, and the one-in-flight guard would block a corrected re-schedule meanwhile.
- **On-chain council rotation** (a `setCouncil`, timelock-gated) — earlier designs kept a mutable council the voter body could rotate in a single proposal, variously gated through `Ownable`'s `onlyOwner` or a bespoke `onlyTimelock` modifier. Rejected: rotation to a _new address_ is rare (a Safe rotates its own signers without changing address), and the one case that needs it — a compromised Safe — is already covered by the voter body revoking the module's timelock roles (disarm) and wiring a fresh module. A privileged mutator and its second access-control path bought no capability the voter body lacked; making the council immutable collapses the module to a single authority (`onlyCouncil`).
- **Bespoke quorum-gated fast cancel for the voter→council direction** (V1's `vetoCouncilUpgrade`) — rejected: reintroduces the vote-counting the OZ Governor migration shed; the Governor-proposal cancel reuses audited code at the cost of a longer council delay.
- **Explicit inactivity gate** (block the council while voters are "active") — rejected: a stock Governor exposes no persistent "active" state; the long cancel window makes the gate emergent — an active voter body cancels, an inactive one lets the upgrade land.

## Consequences

- **A passed voter-body proposal is on-chain-unstoppable.** No actor can cancel a queued voter-body operation once it is in the timelock. The residual defense against a captured voter body is off-chain: the timelock's delay leaves time to coordinate socially, exit, or fork. This is acceptable precisely because voter-body capture is the expensive attack (see the quorum argument above) — the consequence must be revisited if the quorum parameter changes.
- **Inactive-voter honeypot (irreducible).** When the voter body genuinely cannot reach quorum it also cannot cancel, so in the very scenario the council exists for, the council is unchecked apart from the delay. This is the central trust assumption — mitigated only by the delay window and off-chain monitoring, and bounded by voters being able to later replace the council.
- **The council delay is deliberately long** (a full voter cancel cycle + buffer): the council upgrade is *slower* than a voter-body proposal, not faster — its value is liveness (it works when the voter body cannot reach quorum), not speed. Computing it live stops the timing invariant from silently breaking when Governor settings change. For this reason the module's path is called a **backup**, never a *fast-track* — that term stays reserved for V1's genuinely fast `scheduleCouncilUpgrade` (see [ADR-06](./06-governance-agnostic-owner-only-upgradeability.md)/[ADR-02](./02-bind-owner-and-vesting-as-immutables.md)).
- **Double delay on the voter→council cancel.** That cancel is itself a Governor proposal and so passes through the Governor's timelock before it can cancel; this is folded into the council-delay sizing.
- **One upgrade in flight.** The module refuses a council upgrade while another is pending; `cancelUpgrade` frees the slot for a corrected re-schedule. Competing voter-body upgrades are not blocked by the module, but the token's `reinitializer` version guard prevents a stale op from re-running.
- **The council cannot leave on its own.** The council address is immutable and the module has no hand-off or renounce. A council wishing to step down asks the voter body to disarm the module (revoke its roles) and, if a replacement is wanted, wire a fresh one — or it simply stops scheduling upgrades.
